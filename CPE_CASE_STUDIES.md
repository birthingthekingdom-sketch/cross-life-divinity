# Clinical Pastoral Education Case Studies
## Realistic Scenarios for Chaplaincy Practice and Learning

## Case Study 1: Healthcare Chaplaincy - End-of-Life Decisions

### Scenario

Maria Santos is a 78-year-old woman admitted to the hospital with advanced pancreatic cancer. She has been receiving chemotherapy for six months with minimal improvement. Her oncologist has recommended stopping treatment and transitioning to palliative care. Maria's adult children are divided: her son wants to continue aggressive treatment, while her daughter supports stopping treatment. Maria is in significant pain and appears exhausted.

You are called to visit Maria and her family. Maria's daughter says, "Mom wants to stop fighting, but my brother won't listen. Can you help him understand?" Maria's son says, "I'm not ready to give up. There might be clinical trials." Maria herself says quietly, "I'm so tired. I just want to be comfortable."

### Clinical Context

- Maria is Catholic; her faith is important to her
- She has been married for 52 years; her husband is present but quiet
- She has three grandchildren she wants to see graduate
- The family is experiencing significant conflict
- Maria's pain is not well-controlled
- The medical team is waiting for family consensus before changing the treatment plan

### Discussion Questions

1. **Spiritual Assessment:** What spiritual concerns might Maria be experiencing? How would you assess her spiritual needs and resources?

2. **Family Dynamics:** How do you understand the family conflict? What theological or spiritual themes might underlie the disagreement?

3. **Your Role:** What is your role as chaplain in this situation? What are you NOT called to do?

4. **Ethical Considerations:** What ethical principles are at play (autonomy, beneficence, justice)? How do they conflict?

5. **Pastoral Presence:** How would you respond to each family member? What would you say to Maria? To her son? To her daughter?

6. **Boundaries:** What are appropriate boundaries for you as chaplain? Where might you be tempted to overstep?

7. **Self-Awareness:** What personal feelings or experiences might this case trigger for you? How might your own beliefs about death affect your ministry?

8. **Team Collaboration:** How would you communicate with the medical team about the family's needs?

9. **Ritual and Meaning-Making:** What spiritual or religious rituals might support this family? How could you facilitate them?

10. **Ongoing Support:** How would you continue supporting this family after the treatment decision is made?

## Case Study 2: Military Chaplaincy - Combat Trauma and Moral Injury

### Scenario

Captain James Mitchell is a 32-year-old Army officer who recently returned from a 15-month deployment in a combat zone. He served as a squad leader and made tactical decisions that resulted in civilian casualties. While the incident was investigated and deemed within rules of engagement, James struggles with guilt and questions whether his decisions were morally right.

Since returning home, James has been withdrawn, irritable, and having nightmares. He's drinking heavily and has distanced himself from his wife and two young children. His unit commander referred him to you, the military chaplain, noting that James "needs to get his head straight."

James initially resists meeting with you, saying, "I don't need to talk about it. I just need to move on." When you persist gently, he says, "I followed orders. I did my job. But I can't stop thinking about the families."

### Clinical Context

- James comes from a strong Christian faith background
- His wife is concerned about his drinking and emotional distance
- His unit is preparing for another deployment in six months
- There's pressure from command to get James "combat-ready"
- James feels isolated from peers who don't understand his struggle
- He's questioning his faith and God's presence

### Discussion Questions

1. **Moral Injury vs. PTSD:** How do you understand the difference between combat trauma (PTSD) and moral injury? What are the spiritual dimensions?

2. **Theological Crisis:** How might James's faith be affected by his experience? What theological questions might he be wrestling with?

3. **Your Role:** What is your role as military chaplain? How do you balance loyalty to the military with pastoral care for James?

4. **Dual Loyalty:** How do you handle the tension between James's wellbeing and military readiness?

5. **Pastoral Presence:** How would you respond to James's initial resistance? What would you say?

6. **Family Impact:** How might you support James's wife and children? What are their spiritual and emotional needs?

7. **Peer Support:** How could you connect James with other veterans who understand moral injury?

8. **Professional Referral:** When would you refer James to a mental health professional? How would you coordinate care?

9. **Spiritual Resources:** What spiritual practices or resources might help James process his experience?

10. **Long-term Support:** How would you support James if he decides to leave the military? If he stays?

## Case Study 3: Correctional Chaplaincy - Redemption and Justice

### Scenario

Marcus Johnson is a 34-year-old man incarcerated for armed robbery. He is serving a 12-year sentence and has completed 8 years. He has been a model prisoner—participating in education programs, working in the prison kitchen, and mentoring younger inmates. He has become a Christian during his incarceration and is deeply involved in the prison chapel.

Marcus approaches you and says, "Chaplain, I've changed. I'm not the same person who committed that crime. I want to make amends to my victim and to society. I'm applying for parole, and I want to know how to live a Christian life when I get out."

However, you learn that Marcus's victim, Mr. Chen, was severely traumatized by the robbery and has requested that Marcus not be paroled. Mr. Chen has written a victim impact statement expressing his ongoing fear and trauma.

Marcus says, "I understand Mr. Chen's pain. I caused it. But I can't change the past. I can only change the future. How do I live out my faith when I get out?"

### Clinical Context

- Marcus has no family support on the outside
- He has completed GED, vocational training, and anger management
- The prison has limited reentry resources
- Mr. Chen is part of a victim advocacy group
- Marcus's parole hearing is in three months
- He is genuinely seeking spiritual transformation

### Discussion Questions

1. **Redemption and Justice:** How do you understand the tension between redemption (Marcus's transformation) and justice (Mr. Chen's harm and ongoing trauma)?

2. **Authenticity:** How do you assess whether Marcus's spiritual transformation is genuine? What signs would indicate authentic change vs. manipulation?

3. **Victim's Perspective:** How do you honor Mr. Chen's trauma and fear while supporting Marcus's rehabilitation?

4. **Your Role:** What is your role as chaplain? Are you an advocate for Marcus? For the victim? For justice?

5. **Reentry Support:** What spiritual and practical support could you offer Marcus for reentry?

6. **Reconciliation:** Is reconciliation between Marcus and Mr. Chen possible or appropriate? What would that look like?

7. **Community Reintegration:** How would you help a church community welcome Marcus after his release?

8. **Boundaries:** What are appropriate boundaries with Marcus? How do you maintain professional distance while offering pastoral care?

9. **Theological Perspective:** How does your theology of forgiveness, repentance, and justice inform your response?

10. **Systemic Issues:** How do you address systemic injustices in the criminal justice system while supporting individuals?

## Case Study 4: Corporate Chaplaincy - Workplace Crisis

### Scenario

You are a corporate chaplain for a mid-sized manufacturing company. An employee, David, suffered a serious accident on the factory floor and is in critical condition at the hospital. The accident occurred because safety protocols were not followed, and there's already talk of lawsuits and investigations.

The company's HR director asks you to visit David's family at the hospital and "help them understand the company's position" and "prevent them from talking to lawyers." David's coworkers are traumatized and productivity has dropped. Some employees are angry, feeling the company prioritizes profit over safety.

David's wife, Jennifer, is devastated and angry. She says, "This didn't have to happen. The company knew those machines were dangerous. They cut corners to save money." She's also worried about medical bills and lost income.

### Clinical Context

- David is unconscious and on life support
- Jennifer has two young children at home
- David's coworkers witnessed the accident
- The company is facing potential lawsuits
- There's tension between corporate interests and employee wellbeing
- You're being asked to serve corporate interests while providing pastoral care

### Discussion Questions

1. **Conflict of Interest:** How do you navigate the tension between your employer (the company) and your pastoral care responsibilities?

2. **Boundaries:** What are appropriate boundaries in this situation? What should you NOT do?

3. **Pastoral Presence:** How would you support Jennifer without serving corporate interests?

4. **Coworker Support:** How would you support traumatized employees?

5. **Systemic Issues:** How do you address the company's safety failures while maintaining your position?

6. **Advocacy:** When is it appropriate to advocate for employees? What are the limits?

7. **Confidentiality:** What information should remain confidential? What should be shared with the company?

8. **Spiritual Resources:** What spiritual support could you offer Jennifer and the family?

9. **Professional Referral:** When would you refer Jennifer to a lawyer, counselor, or other professional?

10. **Long-term Impact:** How would you support the company and employees in processing this tragedy and preventing future incidents?

## Case Study 5: Interfaith Chaplaincy - Religious Conflict

### Scenario

You are a chaplain in a hospital serving a diverse population. You're called to visit Amira, a 45-year-old Muslim woman admitted with complications from diabetes. Her family has requested that she not be visited by male chaplains and that she have prayer space for daily prayers.

However, the hospital's chaplaincy department is understaffed, and there are no female Muslim chaplains available. The hospital administrator suggests that you (a male Christian chaplain) visit anyway, saying "Just be respectful of her beliefs."

Amira's family is concerned about her spiritual care and whether the hospital respects their faith. The medical team is frustrated with what they perceive as "religious demands" interfering with medical care.

### Clinical Context

- Amira speaks English but prefers Arabic for spiritual matters
- Her family includes her husband, adult children, and extended family
- The hospital has limited resources for interfaith chaplaincy
- There's tension between medical protocols and religious practices
- Amira is anxious about her health and her family's wellbeing
- The hospital has no Muslim chaplain on staff

### Discussion Questions

1. **Cultural Humility:** How do you approach Amira's situation with cultural humility rather than cultural competence?

2. **Boundaries:** What are appropriate boundaries for you as a male Christian chaplain visiting a Muslim woman?

3. **Religious Respect:** How do you honor Amira's religious needs while working within hospital constraints?

4. **Family Involvement:** How do you work with Amira's family to support her spiritual care?

5. **Systemic Change:** What systemic changes would improve interfaith chaplaincy in your hospital?

6. **Collaboration:** How would you collaborate with community Muslim leaders or chaplains?

7. **Medical Team Education:** How would you help the medical team understand the importance of religious practices?

8. **Your Limitations:** What are your honest limitations in serving Amira spiritually?

9. **Advocacy:** When is it appropriate to advocate for Amira's religious needs? What are the limits?

10. **Personal Growth:** What would you need to learn to better serve Muslim patients?

## Case Study 6: Crisis Intervention - Suicide Risk

### Scenario

You're called to the psychiatric unit where Robert, a 52-year-old man, has been admitted after a suicide attempt. He took an overdose of sleeping pills after his wife left him. Robert is medically stable but deeply depressed and hopeless.

Robert says, "There's no point in living. My marriage is over. I'm a failure. My kids don't respect me. I have nothing to live for." He's resistant to treatment and says he'll try again if released.

The psychiatric team is concerned about his safety and is considering involuntary commitment. Robert's ex-wife is angry and blames him for "being selfish." His adult children are confused and hurt.

### Clinical Context

- Robert has no previous psychiatric history
- He has a successful career but feels it's meaningless
- He's isolated from friends and family
- He has no active faith community
- He's resistant to antidepressants
- He's expressing passive suicidal ideation

### Discussion Questions

1. **Spiritual Assessment:** What spiritual factors might be contributing to Robert's hopelessness?

2. **Meaning-Making:** How do you help Robert find meaning and purpose in his suffering?

3. **Crisis Presence:** What is your role in crisis intervention? What are you NOT called to do?

4. **Boundaries:** How do you maintain appropriate boundaries while offering pastoral presence?

5. **Family Support:** How would you support Robert's family?

6. **Professional Collaboration:** How would you work with the psychiatric team?

7. **Spiritual Resources:** What spiritual practices or beliefs might help Robert?

8. **Theology of Suffering:** How does your theology address suffering and hopelessness?

9. **Self-Care:** How would you process the emotional impact of this encounter?

10. **Follow-up:** How would you support Robert after the crisis passes?

## Case Study 7: Cultural Competency - LGBTQ+ Patient Care

### Scenario

You're called to visit Marcus, a 28-year-old gay man admitted with pneumonia. His partner, David, is at his bedside. Marcus is anxious about his care and asks, "Will the staff treat us with respect? I've had bad experiences with healthcare providers who didn't accept my relationship."

You notice that some staff members seem uncomfortable with Marcus and David's physical affection. One nurse refers to David as Marcus's "friend" rather than partner. The chaplain who visited yesterday left a Bible with a note about "repentance."

Marcus says, "I'm a Christian, but I've been rejected by my church because I'm gay. I don't know if God accepts me. I'm scared about my health and about dying without spiritual peace."

### Clinical Context

- Marcus and David have been together for 8 years
- Marcus's biological family is not supportive of his relationship
- Marcus has experienced religious trauma from his church
- He's questioning his faith and God's love
- The hospital has an LGBTQ+ policy but inconsistent implementation
- Marcus is vulnerable due to illness

### Discussion Questions

1. **Affirming Presence:** How do you communicate affirmation and acceptance to Marcus and David?

2. **Religious Trauma:** How do you address Marcus's spiritual wounds from religious rejection?

3. **Theology of Inclusion:** How does your theology address LGBTQ+ identity and relationships?

4. **Staff Education:** How would you address staff discomfort or bias?

5. **Spiritual Resources:** What spiritual support could help Marcus find peace?

6. **Family Dynamics:** How do you support Marcus in the absence of biological family support?

7. **Boundaries:** What are appropriate boundaries in your relationship with Marcus and David?

8. **Advocacy:** When is it appropriate to advocate for LGBTQ+ patients? What are the limits?

9. **Personal Reflection:** What are your own beliefs and biases regarding LGBTQ+ identity?

10. **Systemic Change:** What systemic changes would improve LGBTQ+ inclusion in healthcare chaplaincy?

## Using These Case Studies

### For Individual Reflection

- Read the scenario and discussion questions
- Spend 20-30 minutes reflecting on how you would respond
- Journal your thoughts, feelings, and questions
- Identify areas for growth and learning

### For Group Discussion

- Present the scenario to a group of chaplains or students
- Discuss different perspectives and approaches
- Share experiences from your own practice
- Develop collaborative responses

### For Supervision

- Bring a case study to supervision
- Discuss how it relates to your clinical practice
- Explore your emotional reactions and biases
- Develop skills and strategies

### For Training

- Use case studies to teach chaplaincy competencies
- Develop role-plays based on scenarios
- Practice difficult conversations
- Build confidence and skills

## Reflection Questions for All Cases

Regardless of the specific case, consider these universal questions:

1. **Spiritual Assessment:** What are the patient's/family's spiritual needs, beliefs, and resources?

2. **Your Role:** What is your role as chaplain? What are you NOT called to do?

3. **Boundaries:** What are appropriate professional boundaries?

4. **Self-Awareness:** What personal feelings, biases, or experiences does this case trigger?

5. **Theological Reflection:** How does your theology inform your response?

6. **Ethical Considerations:** What ethical principles are at play? How do they conflict?

7. **Cultural Competency:** What cultural factors are important to understand?

8. **Team Collaboration:** How would you work with the healthcare or institutional team?

9. **Advocacy:** When is it appropriate to advocate? What are the limits?

10. **Self-Care:** How would you process the emotional impact of this encounter?
