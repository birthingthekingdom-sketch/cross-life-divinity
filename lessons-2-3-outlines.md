### Part I: Biblical Foundation for Contextualization

#### Scripture Study: 1 Corinthians 9:19-23

**Full Text Written Out** (NKJV):
> <sup>19</sup> For though I am free from all men, I have made myself a servant to all, that I might win the more; <sup>20</sup> and to the Jews I became as a Jew, that I might win Jews; to those who are under the law, as under the law, that I might win those who are under the law; <sup>21</sup> to those who are without law, as without law (not being without law toward God, but under law toward Christ), that I might win those who are without law; <sup>22</sup> to the weak I became as weak, that I might win the weak. I have become all things to all men, that I might by all means save some. <sup>23</sup> Now this I do for the gospel's sake, that I may be partaker of it with you.

**Verse-by-Verse Commentary**:

**Verse 19**: "I have made myself a servant to all"
- Greek: **δουλόω** (*douloō*) - "to enslave oneself, to become a servant"
- Paul voluntarily surrendered his rights and privileges
- Purpose: **ἵνα** (*hina*) - "in order that" - purposeful strategy
- Goal: "win the more" - maximize gospel impact

**Verse 20-21**: Cultural Adaptation Examples
- **To Jews**: Observed Jewish customs when among Jews (Acts 16:3 - circumcision of Timothy)
- **To those under law**: Respected Mosaic regulations without being bound by them
- **To Gentiles**: Lived freely from Jewish ceremonial law
- **Critical qualifier**: "not being without law toward God, but under law toward Christ"
  - Paul never compromised moral law or gospel truth
  - Adaptation was in **method**, not **message**

**Verse 22**: "I have become all things to all men"
- This is **NOT** relativism or compromise
- This **IS** cultural sensitivity and strategic communication
- Greek: **πάντα γέγονα** (*panta gegona*) - perfect tense: "I have become and remain"

**Verse 23**: "For the gospel's sake"
- Ultimate motivation: advancing the gospel
- Personal benefit: "partaker" - sharing in gospel blessings
- Theological foundation: Gospel is non-negotiable; methods are flexible

**Theological Implications**:
1. **Message vs. Method**: The gospel content is fixed; communication style adapts
2. **Incarnational Ministry**: Following Christ's example of entering human culture
3. **Missional Flexibility**: Willingness to sacrifice personal preferences for gospel advance

---

#### Scripture Study: Acts 17:16-34 (Paul at Athens)

**Full Text Written Out** (Selected verses with commentary)

**Verses 16-17**: Paul's Burden and Initial Approach
> <sup>16</sup> Now while Paul waited for them at Athens, his spirit was provoked within him when he saw that the city was given over to idols. <sup>17</sup> Therefore he reasoned in the synagogue with the Jews and with the Gentile worshipers, and in the marketplace daily with those who happened to be there.

**Commentary**:
- **"Provoked" (παρωξύνετο, *parōxyneto*)**: Deeply distressed, stirred to action
- Paul's response: **Engagement**, not isolation
- Two venues: Synagogue (religious) and Agora (secular marketplace)
- Method: **"Reasoned" (διελέγετο, *dielegeto*)** - dialogue, discussion, persuasion

**Verses 22-23**: Cultural Connection Point
> <sup>22</sup> Then Paul stood in the midst of the Areopagus and said, "Men of Athens, I perceive that in all things you are very religious; <sup>23</sup> for as I was passing through and considering the objects of your worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: TO THE UNKNOWN GOD. Therefore, the One whom you worship without knowing, Him I proclaim to you"

**Commentary**:
- **Strategic opening**: Affirms their religiosity (not idolatry)
- **Cultural observation**: Paul studied their culture before speaking
- **Bridge concept**: "Unknown God" - moves from their worldview to biblical truth
- **Proclamation**: Clear transition to gospel truth

**Verses 24-31**: Gospel Presentation (Adapted for Philosophical Audience)
- **v. 24-25**: Creator God (challenges Greek polytheism)
- **v. 26-27**: God's sovereignty and human seeking
- **v. 28**: Quotes Greek poets (Epimenides and Aratus) - uses their own literature
- **v. 29**: Challenges idolatry using logic
- **v. 30-31**: Calls for repentance and proclaims resurrection

**Key Principles**:
1. **Cultural awareness**: Paul knew Greek philosophy and poetry
2. **Common ground**: Started with shared beliefs before challenging errors
3. **Logical argumentation**: Appealed to reason (appropriate for philosophers)
4. **Clear gospel**: Despite cultural adaptation, resurrection and repentance were explicit

### Part II: Understanding Cultural Barriers

#### Major Categories of Barriers

**1. Intellectual Barriers**
- Philosophical objections (naturalism, relativism, pluralism)
- Scientific skepticism
- Problem of evil and suffering
- Biblical reliability questions

**Scripture**: 2 Corinthians 10:4-5
> "For the weapons of our warfare are not carnal but mighty in God for pulling down strongholds, casting down arguments and every high thing that exalts itself against the knowledge of God"

**2. Cultural Barriers**
- Religious background (Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, secularism)
- Ethnic and linguistic differences
- Social class and economic status
- Political ideologies

**Scripture**: Galatians 3:28
> "There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is neither male nor female; for you are all one in Christ Jesus"

**3. Emotional Barriers**
- Past hurt by Christians or churches
- Fear of rejection by family/community
- Guilt and shame
- Pride and self-sufficiency

**Scripture**: 2 Timothy 2:24-26
> "And a servant of the Lord must not quarrel but be gentle to all, able to teach, patient, in humility correcting those who are in opposition"

---

### Part III: Practical Contextualization Strategies

#### Strategy 1: Know Your Audience (Exegete the Culture)

**Steps**:
1. **Research**: Study the worldview, values, and beliefs of your target audience
2. **Listen**: Ask questions before making statements
3. **Observe**: Identify felt needs and cultural touchpoints
4. **Adapt**: Adjust communication style (not content) accordingly

**Biblical Example**: Jesus with the Samaritan woman (John 4)
- Broke cultural barriers (gender, ethnicity, moral status)
- Started with physical need (water)
- Moved to spiritual need (living water)
- Confronted sin graciously
- Revealed Himself as Messiah

#### Strategy 2: Find Bridge Concepts

**Definition**: Cultural or philosophical concepts that can serve as entry points for gospel truth

**Examples**:
- **To Muslims**: Emphasize Jesus as prophet, then reveal His deity
- **To Hindus**: Discuss karma and introduce grace
- **To secularists**: Address meaning, purpose, and morality
- **To postmodernists**: Emphasize personal relationship over propositional truth

**Caution**: Bridge concepts are **starting points**, not endpoints. Must lead to full gospel.

#### Strategy 3: Address Objections with Gentleness

**1 Peter 3:15-16**:
> "But sanctify the Lord God in your hearts, and always be ready to give a defense to everyone who asks you a reason for the hope that is in you, with meekness and fear; having a good conscience"

**Key Elements**:
- **Preparation**: "Always be ready" - study apologetics
- **Attitude**: "Meekness and fear" - humility and reverence
- **Method**: "Give a defense" (ἀπολογία, *apologia*) - reasoned explanation

---

### Part IV: Case Studies in Contextualization

#### Case Study 1: Urban Secular Professional
- **Worldview**: Naturalistic, values science and reason
- **Barriers**: Sees Christianity as anti-intellectual
- **Approach**: Evidential apologetics, historical reliability of resurrection
- **Bridge**: Discuss meaning and purpose in life

#### Case Study 2: Traditional Muslim
- **Worldview**: Monotheistic, values honor and family
- **Barriers**: Sees Trinity as polytheism, Bible as corrupted
- **Approach**: Emphasize common ground (one God, prophets, moral law)
- **Bridge**: Jesus in the Quran, then Jesus in the Bible

#### Case Study 3: Postmodern College Student
- **Worldview**: Relativistic, values authenticity and tolerance
- **Barriers**: Rejects absolute truth claims
- **Approach**: Narrative theology, personal testimony, relational authenticity
- **Bridge**: Discuss exclusive truth claims in all worldviews

---

### Part V: Maintaining Gospel Integrity

#### Non-Negotiables in Contextualization

**What CAN Change**:
- Communication style (formal vs. casual)
- Illustrations and examples
- Starting points and bridge concepts
- Order of presentation
- Cultural expressions (music, art, dress)

**What CANNOT Change**:
- Human sinfulness (Romans 3:23)
- Christ's deity and humanity
- Substitutionary atonement (1 Corinthians 15:3-4)
- Resurrection (1 Corinthians 15:14)
- Salvation by grace through faith (Ephesians 2:8-9)
- Call to repentance (Acts 17:30)

**Warning**: Syncretism
- **Definition**: Blending Christianity with other religions or philosophies
- **Biblical Condemnation**: Galatians 1:6-9
- **Example**: Judaizers in early church (Acts 15)

---

### Conclusion

Contextualized evangelism is not compromise but **strategic communication**. Like Paul, we must become "all things to all people" while never diluting the gospel message. Cultural sensitivity demonstrates love; theological clarity demonstrates faithfulness.

### Opening Statement (300 words)

**Welcome Message**: Introduction to prayer as the foundation of Christian life and ministry

**Learning Objectives**:

### Part I: The Nature of Prayer

#### What is Prayer?

**Biblical Definition**: Prayer is **communication with God** - speaking to Him, listening to Him, and aligning our will with His.

**Theological Foundation**:
- Prayer presupposes a **personal God** who hears and responds
- Prayer is rooted in **relationship**, not ritual
- Prayer is both a **privilege** (access to God) and a **responsibility** (commanded by God)

#### Scripture Study: 1 Thessalonians 5:16-18

**Full Text** (NKJV):
> <sup>16</sup> Rejoice always, <sup>17</sup> pray without ceasing, <sup>18</sup> in everything give thanks; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you.

**Commentary**:

**Verse 17**: "Pray without ceasing"
- Greek: **ἀδιαλείπτως** (*adialeiptōs*) - "without intermission, constantly"
- This does NOT mean 24/7 verbal prayer
- This DOES mean **continual attitude** of prayerful dependence on God
- Comparison: Breathing - constant, natural, life-sustaining

**Theological Implication**: Prayer is not an isolated activity but a **lifestyle of communion** with God.

---

### Part II: The Lord's Prayer - Our Model

#### Scripture Study: Matthew 6:9-13

**Full Text** (NKJV):
> <sup>9</sup> In this manner, therefore, pray: Our Father in heaven, Hallowed be Your name. <sup>10</sup> Your kingdom come. Your will be done On earth as it is in heaven. <sup>11</sup> Give us this day our daily bread. <sup>12</sup> And forgive us our debts, As we forgive our debtors. <sup>13</sup> And do not lead us into temptation, But deliver us from the evil one. For Yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever. Amen.

**Structure of the Lord's Prayer**:

#### Part A: Upward Focus (God-Centered)

**"Our Father in heaven"**
- **Intimacy**: "Father" (Aramaic: Abba) - personal relationship
- **Transcendence**: "in heaven" - God's majesty and sovereignty
- **Community**: "Our" - corporate, not individualistic

**"Hallowed be Your name"**
- Greek: **ἁγιασθήτω** (*hagiasthētō*) - "let it be sanctified, revered"
- **Adoration**: Worship of God's character
- Priority: God's glory comes before our needs

**"Your kingdom come, Your will be done"**
- **Submission**: Aligning our desires with God's purposes
- **Eschatological**: Praying for Christ's return and reign
- **Present application**: God's will "on earth as it is in heaven"

#### Part B: Inward Focus (Our Needs)

**"Give us this day our daily bread"**
- **Dependence**: Acknowledging God as provider
- **Present tense**: "this day" - daily trust, not hoarding
- **Physical and spiritual**: "Bread" includes all necessities

**"Forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors"**
- **Confession**: Admitting our sin
- **Condition**: Our forgiveness of others reflects God's forgiveness of us (Matthew 6:14-15)
- **Relational**: Sin affects our relationship with God and others

**"Lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil"**
- **Protection**: Asking God to guard us from sin
- **Deliverance**: Rescue from Satan's schemes
- Greek: **πονηροῦ** (*ponērou*) - "the evil one" (personal devil)

**Doxology**: "For Yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever"
- **Conclusion**: Returns to worship
- **Confidence**: God has authority and ability to answer

---

### Part III: The ACTS Model of Prayer

#### A - Adoration

**Definition**: Praising God for **who He is** (His attributes and character)

**Scripture**: Psalm 103:1-5
> "Bless the LORD, O my soul; And all that is within me, bless His holy name!"

**Examples of Adoration**:
- God, You are holy, righteous, and just
- You are eternal, unchanging, and faithful
- You are loving, merciful, and gracious

**Why Start with Adoration?**
- Puts us in proper posture (humility)
- Reminds us of God's greatness
- Shifts focus from self to God

---

#### C - Confession

**Definition**: Admitting our sins and seeking God's forgiveness

**Scripture**: 1 John 1:9
> "If we confess our sins, He is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."

**Greek Word Study**:
- **"Confess" (ὁμολογέω, *homologeō*)**: "to say the same thing" - agreeing with God about our sin

**Elements of Biblical Confession**:
1. **Specificity**: Name specific sins, not vague generalities
2. **Responsibility**: Own your sin without excuses
3. **Repentance**: Turn from sin, not just feel sorry

---

#### T - Thanksgiving

**Definition**: Expressing gratitude for **what God has done**

**Scripture**: Philippians 4:6
> "Be anxious for nothing, but in everything by prayer and supplication, with thanksgiving, let your requests be made known to God"

**Distinction**:
- **Adoration**: Who God is
- **Thanksgiving**: What God has done

**Benefits of Thanksgiving**:
- Cultivates contentment
- Combats anxiety (Philippians 4:6-7)
- Increases faith by remembering God's faithfulness

---

#### S - Supplication

**Definition**: Making requests for ourselves and others (intercession)

**Scripture**: Philippians 4:6 (above)

**Types of Supplication**:
1. **Petition**: Requests for personal needs
2. **Intercession**: Requests for others

**Biblical Intercession Examples**:
- Abraham interceding for Sodom (Genesis 18:22-33)
- Moses interceding for Israel (Exodus 32:11-14)
- Paul interceding for churches (Ephesians 1:15-23, 3:14-21)

---

### Part IV: Biblical Examples of Powerful Prayer

#### Example 1: Hannah's Prayer (1 Samuel 1:9-20)

**Context**: Barren woman desperate for a child

**Prayer Elements**:
- **Honesty**: Poured out her soul in bitterness (v. 10)
- **Vow**: Promised to dedicate child to God (v. 11)
- **Persistence**: Prayed fervently (v. 12)
- **Faith**: Went away no longer sad (v. 18)

**Result**: God gave her Samuel, who became a great prophet

**Lesson**: God hears desperate, honest prayer

---

#### Example 2: Elijah on Mount Carmel (1 Kings 18:36-39)

**Context**: Confrontation with prophets of Baal

**Prayer Elements**:
- **Public boldness**: Prayed before crowd
- **God's glory**: "Let it be known that You are God" (v. 36)
- **Simple and direct**: Short, clear request

**Result**: Fire fell from heaven, people declared "The LORD, He is God!"

**Lesson**: Prayer that seeks God's glory is powerful

---

#### Example 3: Jesus in Gethsemane (Matthew 26:36-46)

**Context**: Hours before crucifixion

**Prayer Elements**:
- **Submission**: "Not My will, but Yours" (v. 39)
- **Honesty**: "My soul is exceedingly sorrowful" (v. 38)
- **Persistence**: Prayed three times (v. 39, 42, 44)

**Result**: Strengthened to face the cross

**Lesson**: Prayer aligns our will with God's, even in suffering

---

### Part V: Hindrances to Prayer

#### Hindrance 1: Unconfessed Sin

**Scripture**: Psalm 66:18
> "If I regard iniquity in my heart, The Lord will not hear"

**Solution**: Regular confession (1 John 1:9)

#### Hindrance 2: Unforgiveness

**Scripture**: Mark 11:25
> "And whenever you stand praying, if you have anything against anyone, forgive him, that your Father in heaven may also forgive you your trespasses"

**Solution**: Reconcile relationships before praying

#### Hindrance 3: Wrong Motives

**Scripture**: James 4:3
> "You ask and do not receive, because you ask amiss, that you may spend it on your pleasures"

**Solution**: Pray for God's will, not selfish desires

#### Hindrance 4: Lack of Faith

**Scripture**: James 1:6-7
> "But let him ask in faith, with no doubting, for he who doubts is like a wave of the sea driven and tossed by the wind. For let not that man suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord"

**Solution**: Trust God's character and promises

---

### Conclusion

Prayer is not a religious duty but a **relational privilege**. Through prayer, we commune with the Creator of the universe, align our will with His, and participate in His work on earth. The Lord's Prayer provides a model; the ACTS framework provides structure; biblical examples provide inspiration. In the next lesson, we will explore intercession - praying for others.
