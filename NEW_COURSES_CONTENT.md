# New M.Div. Equivalent Courses - Content Development

## 1. CAPSTONE PROJECT

### Course Overview
The Capstone Project is the culminating experience in the Master of Divinity program, designed to synthesize theological learning, integrate practical ministry experience, and demonstrate scholarly competency in a focused area of divinity studies.

### Course Description
Students will develop and complete a substantial independent project that demonstrates mastery of theological content, research methodology, and professional ministry application. This capstone serves as a bridge between academic study and vocational ministry, allowing students to apply their theological education to real-world ministry contexts or scholarly inquiry.

### Learning Outcomes
By completion of this course, students will:
- Synthesize theological knowledge from across the M.Div. curriculum
- Conduct rigorous theological research using primary and secondary sources
- Develop a significant project that demonstrates professional competency in ministry or theological scholarship
- Present findings in a format appropriate to their ministry context (thesis, ministry plan, curriculum development, etc.)
- Reflect critically on their theological formation and vocational calling

### Course Structure
- **Duration:** 8-16 weeks (depending on project scope)
- **Credit Hours:** 3-6 credits
- **Prerequisites:** Completion of core M.Div. courses (Old Testament Survey, New Testament Survey, Church History Survey, Christology, Homiletics/Preaching)
- **Format:** Independent study with faculty mentorship and peer review

### Project Options
1. **Theological Thesis:** Original research on a theological topic (15,000-20,000 words)
2. **Ministry Project:** Practical application project (curriculum, program design, community initiative)
3. **Scholarly Article:** Publication-ready article for peer-reviewed journal (8,000-12,000 words)
4. **Integrated Project:** Combination of research, creative work, and practical application

### Assessment Criteria
- Theological depth and originality
- Research quality and source documentation
- Professional presentation and writing
- Integration of M.Div. learning
- Practical applicability to ministry context

## 2. CHRISTOLOGY

### Course Overview
Christology is a systematic study of the person and work of Jesus Christ, examining biblical foundations, historical development of Christian doctrine, and contemporary theological perspectives on Christ's incarnation, atonement, resurrection, and significance for Christian faith and practice.

### Course Description
This course explores the central Christian claim that Jesus Christ is God incarnate and investigates how this claim has been understood, defended, and applied throughout Christian history. Students will examine biblical witness to Christ, major christological controversies and councils, diverse theological traditions (Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant, Evangelical), and contemporary challenges to classical Christology. The course emphasizes both doctrinal precision and pastoral relevance.

### Learning Outcomes
By completion of this course, students will:
- Articulate biblical foundations for Christian understanding of Christ's person and work
- Trace the historical development of christological doctrine from the early church through contemporary theology
- Analyze major christological models and their theological implications
- Evaluate contemporary challenges and alternative Christologies
- Apply christological understanding to Christian worship, ethics, and mission

### Course Structure
- **Duration:** 12-16 weeks (one semester)
- **Credit Hours:** 3-4 credits
- **Prerequisites:** Old Testament Survey, New Testament Survey, Church History Survey (or equivalent)
- **Format:** Lecture, discussion, research papers, presentations

### Major Topics
1. **Biblical Foundations:** Messianic expectation in OT; Jesus' self-understanding; apostolic proclamation of Christ
2. **Early Church Development:** Nicene Christology; Council of Chalcedon; Monophysite and Nestorian controversies
3. **Medieval and Reformation Perspectives:** Anselm's satisfaction theory; Aquinas on incarnation; Luther and Calvin on Christ
4. **Modern Theological Perspectives:** Liberal theology; Barth's Christology; Process theology; Feminist Christology
5. **Contemporary Issues:** Pluralism and Christ; Christology in non-Western contexts; Christology and science
6. **Pastoral Application:** Christ in preaching, worship, counseling, and Christian living

### Key Texts and Resources
- Biblical texts (Gospels, Hebrews, Colossians, John)
- Patristic writings (Athanasius, Cyril of Alexandria, John of Damascus)
- Medieval theology (Anselm, Aquinas)
- Modern theology (Barth, Tillich, Rahner)
- Contemporary works (Johnson, Torrance, Kienzle)

### Assessment Methods
- Exams (midterm and final)
- Research papers (2-3 papers, 3,000-5,000 words each)
- Class participation and discussion
- Presentation on contemporary Christology

## 3. CONTEMPORARY THEOLOGICAL ISSUES

### Course Overview
This seminar explores pressing theological questions and challenges facing the contemporary church, including issues of justice, pluralism, technology, environmental stewardship, gender and sexuality, interfaith dialogue, and the role of Christianity in a secular society. Students engage current theological discourse and develop informed Christian responses.

### Course Description
The church exists in a rapidly changing world facing unprecedented challenges and opportunities. This course examines how classical Christian theology addresses contemporary issues while remaining open to new insights. Students will study major contemporary theological movements (liberation theology, feminist theology, womanist theology, queer theology, creation care theology) and their engagement with specific issues. The course emphasizes both prophetic critique and constructive theological work.

### Learning Outcomes
By completion of this course, students will:
- Identify and analyze major theological issues facing the contemporary church
- Understand diverse theological perspectives on contemporary challenges
- Develop informed theological responses grounded in Christian tradition and contemporary context
- Engage in constructive theological dialogue across differences
- Apply theological reflection to personal and communal Christian practice

### Course Structure
- **Duration:** 12-16 weeks (one semester)
- **Credit Hours:** 3-4 credits
- **Prerequisites:** Core M.Div. courses (Old Testament Survey, New Testament Survey, Church History Survey)
- **Format:** Seminar with student presentations, theological debates, case study analysis

### Major Topics (Rotating Emphasis)
1. **Justice and Reconciliation:** Liberation theology, racial justice, economic justice, reparations
2. **Pluralism and Interfaith Dialogue:** Religious diversity, Christian exclusivism/inclusivism/pluralism, interfaith cooperation
3. **Gender, Sexuality, and Identity:** Feminist theology, LGBTQ+ inclusion, gender justice, embodiment
4. **Environmental Theology:** Creation care, climate justice, ecological ethics, sustainability
5. **Technology and Digital Ethics:** Artificial intelligence, social media, virtual community, digital justice
6. **Church and Society:** Secularization, political theology, prophetic witness, Christian activism
7. **Global Christianity:** Majority World theology, postcolonial perspectives, indigenous Christianity

### Pedagogical Approach
- **Case Studies:** Real-world scenarios requiring theological analysis
- **Theological Debates:** Structured discussions of contested issues
- **Guest Speakers:** Practitioners and scholars engaged in contemporary ministry
- **Student Research:** Individual and group projects on specific issues
- **Interfaith Engagement:** Dialogue with representatives from other faith traditions (when appropriate)

### Assessment Methods
- Theological reflection papers (2-3 papers, 2,000-3,000 words each)
- Seminar participation and discussion
- Student presentations on contemporary issues
- Final project (research paper, creative project, or action plan)

### Significance for M.Div. Formation
This course prepares graduates for prophetic, contextual ministry in the 21st century. It develops theological imagination, critical thinking, and pastoral sensitivity to the concerns of their communities. Graduates will be equipped to address contemporary challenges with theological depth and pastoral wisdom.

## INTEGRATION NOTES

### Alignment with M.Div. Standards
All three new courses align with Association of Theological Schools (ATS) standards for Master of Divinity programs:
- **Capstone Project:** Culminating experience requirement
- **Christology:** Core theological content (Christology/soteriology)
- **Contemporary Theological Issues:** Contextual theology and prophetic witness

### Transfer Pathway Support
These courses, combined with existing offerings (Old Testament Survey, New Testament Survey, Homiletics/Preaching, Church History Survey), provide comprehensive M.Div. equivalent preparation for:
- Transfer to accredited M.Div. programs
- Ordination candidacy in various denominations
- Advanced theological study
- Professional ministry roles

### Prerequisite Structure
- **Foundation:** Old Testament Survey, New Testament Survey, Church History Survey
- **Core Theology:** Christology, Contemporary Theological Issues
- **Ministry Skills:** Homiletics/Preaching
- **Capstone:** Integrates all previous learning

### Recommended Sequence
**Year 1:**
- Old Testament Survey
- New Testament Survey
- Church History Survey

**Year 2:**
- Christology
- Homiletics/Preaching
- Contemporary Theological Issues

**Year 3:**
- Capstone Project (with ongoing electives/specializations)
